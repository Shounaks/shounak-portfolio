There is a particular kind of silence that every on-call engineer dreads. Not the silence of a quiet night — the silence of a system that has stopped talking. No logs. No traces. No metrics. Just a wall of nothing, and somewhere out there, users staring at a spinner that will never resolve.

This guide exists because of that silence. It is a complete walkthrough of observability for production systems — particularly LLM-backed systems — starting from the absolute basics and building up to the kind of multi-layered, burn-rate-driven alerting strategy that catches incidents before they become catastrophes. By the end, you will have a mental model for why every piece of observability infrastructure exists, and how to build it properly the first time.

Let's start at the beginning.

---

## Part I: First Principles — What Is Observability and Why Should You Care?

Observability is not dashboards. Observability is not alerts. Observability is the property of a system that lets you understand its internal state from its external outputs. Dashboards and alerts are tools; observability is the capability they give you.

Every observability system rests on three pillars:

### Metrics — "What is happening right now, quantitatively?"

A metric is a number over time. It answers questions like: *What is our error rate right now? How many requests did we serve in the last minute? How full is our thread pool?* Metrics are cheap to store, cheap to query, and cheap to alert on. They give you the 30,000-foot view.

In a Java stack, metrics flow through **Micrometer** and land in **SignalFx** (or Datadog, Prometheus, etc.). The key metric primitives are:

<TableCSV source="Instrument| Explaination
Counter | counts how many times X happened. It always goes up. You derive a *rate* from it (errors per second). Examples: request count, error count, cache misses.
Timer  | measures how long X took. It automatically tracks count, sum, and max, which lets you derive percentiles like p95 and p99. Examples: HTTP response time, LLM call duration, database query latency.
Gauge | a point-in-time reading that can go up or down. Examples: thread count, queue depth, active connections. Unlike a counter, a gauge does not accumulate.
" />

### Logs — "What happened in this specific request?"

A log is a record of a single event. It answers questions like: *Why did request `abc-123` fail? What was the state of the system when the error occurred? Which user triggered this code path?*

Logs are not metrics. They are not time-series. They are rich, contextual records that you search when you need to understand a specific incident. Tools like **Splunk** let you search structured logs with queries like `requestId=abc-123 AND status=500`.

The value of logs is in their richness. A good log line tells you: who made the request, what they asked for, what happened, how long it took, and what the system state was at that moment. A bad log line tells you `ERROR occurred`. We will revisit what to log later in this guide.

### Traces — "How did a request travel through the system?"

A trace follows a single request across every service it touched. Where metrics tell you the aggregate picture and logs tell you about individual events, traces give you the *causal chain*. They answer: *We know request `abc-123` was slow. Was it slow because of the LLM call, the Redis write, the database query, or the network hop between services?*

**OpenTelemetry** has become the standard for distributed tracing. A trace is built from spans — each span represents one unit of work in one service, with a start time, duration, and parent span ID. 

Together, they form a tree: `Request → Controller → LLM Call (3.2s) → Redis Write (0.1s) → Response`.

### The Monitoring Pyramid

These three pillars support a four-level monitoring pyramid:

```
Level 1: Business KPIs & SLO Burn Rate    ← Are users succeeding?
Level 2: Application Metrics & Errors     ← Is the app healthy?
Level 3: Infrastructure Health Metrics    ← Are the resources OK?
Level 4: Synthetic Probes                 ← Can users reach us at all?
```

This pyramid has a crucial implication for alerting strategy: **alerts should fire from top to bottom**. An SLO burn rate alert means users are *already* impacted. A thread exhaustion alert means users *will be* impacted soon. The pyramid tells you the severity of every signal before you even look at the data.

## Part II: The Four Golden Signals — Google's Gift to SREs

Google's Site Reliability Engineering team distilled years of production experience into four metrics that, together, describe the health of almost any service. Every other metric is a derivative or a diagnostic tool. These four are universal.

### 1. Latency — How long does a request take?

Latency is the most user-visible signal. A service that returns errors is broken. A service that returns correct responses in 30 seconds is also broken — users just experience it differently. Latency often degrades *before* errors start appearing, making it an early warning system for incidents that haven't fully manifested yet.

Critical nuance: **measure latency at the right percentile**. Average latency is almost useless because it masks the tail. A service with mean latency of 200ms might have p99 latency of 8 seconds — one in a hundred users waiting 40x longer than they expect. Always alert on p95 and p99.

### 2. Traffic — How much demand is on the system?

Traffic is the baseline against which everything else is measured. An error rate of 5 errors/minute means something very different at 50 requests/minute versus 50,000 requests/minute. Traffic also reveals *zero traffic* — a complete absence of requests that often signals a catastrophic failure (crashed service, DNS failure, load balancer misconfiguration) that produces no errors because nothing is reaching the system to fail.

### 3. Errors — What fraction of requests are failing?

Errors are the most direct signal of user impact. They are also the most seductive signal to alert on naively — and we will spend a significant portion of this guide explaining why naive error rate alerting is broken and how to fix it.

### 4. Saturation — How "full" is the service?

Saturation measures how close the system is to its limits. Thread pools, connection pools, memory, CPU — every resource has a ceiling, and saturation tells you how far below that ceiling you are. The value of saturation metrics is *predictive*: they fire before users see impact, giving you a window to act.

## Part III: The Observability Stack for LLM Systems

LLM-backed systems introduce new dimensions of observability complexity that don't exist in traditional web services. A request to a conventional API takes milliseconds or a few seconds. An LLM call takes seconds to tens of seconds, streams partial responses, can fail silently in the middle of generation, and consumes variable numbers of tokens that directly translate to cost. Conventional observability strategies break down here.

The full observability stack for an LLM system looks like this:

**Metrics (Micrometer → SignalFx)** — counters, timers, gauges, and histograms. Custom app metrics for LLM-specific signals like token usage, model selection, and streaming health.

**Dashboards (SignalFx via Terraform)** — visual charts for real-time exploration. Domain-specific dashboards for different concerns: agent operations, workflow execution, tool calls, knowledge base retrieval, batch evaluation.

**Detectors / Alerts** — automated signals when metrics cross thresholds or burn rates exceed acceptable levels.

**SLOs (TOME Terraform)** — formal Service Level Objective definitions with burn rate monitoring. The heart of user-impact-driven alerting.

**Error Tracking (Sentry)** — exception tracking, grouping, and fingerprinting. Where you go after an alert fires to understand *why*.

**Synthetic Monitoring** — scheduled probes that verify end-to-end user flows work, independent of real traffic.

## Part IV: The Four Pillars of Application Metrics

With the stack in place, let's go deep on what to actually measure. There are four pillars, each addressing a different dimension of system health.

---

### Pillar 1: Endpoint Reliability (Error Rate)

**What it measures:** The percentage of HTTP requests that return a non-2xx, non-5xx response. In plain language: how often are users receiving failures?

**Why it matters:** This is the most direct translation of "is the service doing its job?" When reliability drops, conversations fail to load, agents cannot be created, and chat messages stop streaming. It is the signal closest to actual user experience.

**How it is measured:** Standard HTTP metrics tagged by service ID, endpoint path, and HTTP method. But here is where most teams make their first mistake — they alert on raw error rate, and that is a trap.

The naive threshold approach says: *"Alert me if error rate exceeds 1%."* This sounds reasonable. It is not. There are three fundamental problems:

**Problem 1: Noise at low volume.** One error out of 50 requests is a 2% error rate. Should that page an on-call engineer? Almost certainly not. It might be a transient network blip, a single misbehaving client, a flaky integration test.

**Problem 2: Deafness at high volume.** 500 errors out of 100,000 requests is a 0.5% error rate. No page fires. But 500 real users just had their requests fail. That is not acceptable silence.

**Problem 3: No budget awareness.** The raw error rate tells you nothing about whether this rate is sustainable for your monthly reliability target. Is 0.8% error rate a problem? Depends entirely on your SLO.

The solution is the **Burn Rate Model**.

---

## Part V: The Burn Rate Model — The Heart of Modern Alerting

This is the most important section of this guide. If you only internalize one concept, make it this one.

### What is an Error Budget?

If your SLO is 99.5% reliability over 30 days, you have agreed to be reliable 99.5% of the time. The 0.5% you are *allowed* to fail is your **error budget**. Numerically:

```
Error Budget = (1 - SLO) × Total Time
             = 0.5% × 30 days
             = 0.5% × 43,200 minutes
             = 216 minutes of allowed downtime per month
```

That is it. 216 minutes. Every minute you are degraded beyond your SLO, you are spending from that budget. When the budget hits zero, you have broken your SLO commitment. The question is not "are we in error right now" — the question is "how fast are we *spending* the budget we have?"

### What is Burn Rate?

Burn rate measures how fast you are spending your error budget relative to the *steady state* allowed rate.

```
Burn Rate = Observed Error Rate / Budget Error Rate
```

If your SLO is 99.5%, your budget error rate is 0.5%. If your observed error rate is 2.5%:

```
Burn Rate = 2.5% / 0.5% = 5×
```

You are burning your error budget **five times faster than allowed**. At this rate, you will exhaust 30 days of error budget in 6 days.

This is the insight that makes burn rate so powerful: it translates abstract error rates into concrete, time-bounded impact. A burn rate of 9× means you are burning through a full month of error budget in roughly 3.3 days. That is a P1 incident in disguise.

### The Multi-Window Approach: Catching Every Failure Mode

Different failure modes burn budget at different speeds, and a single detection window cannot catch all of them. The solution is a **dual-window detector** for each burn rate tier:

<TableCSV source="| Tier | Burn Rate | Long Window | Short Window | Min Failures | Severity |
| Steep | 9× | 46 minutes | 4 minutes | 5 | MAJOR |
| Moderate | 5× | 5h 36m | 28 minutes | 5 | MAJOR |
| Slow | 1× | 2d 19h | 5h 36m | 5 | MINOR | " />

- **Steep (9×):** A severe incident. A bad deployment causing 18% errors. The short window fires in 4 minutes — fast enough to catch it before most users are impacted.

- **Moderate (5×):** Significant degradation. Intermittent LLM failures causing 10% errors. Detected in about 20 minutes.

- **Slow (1×):** A slow, silent leak. A 2% error rate seeping for hours, slowly eroding your monthly budget without triggering any threshold-based alert.

The **dual window requirement** is the key insight: *both* the long and short windows must fire simultaneously. This prevents two failure modes:

- **Long window alone** delays alerting on sudden spikes — you could be burning at 9× for 20 minutes before the 46-minute window fires.
- **Short window alone** generates noise on brief blips — a 30-second retry storm looks terrifying in a 4-minute window.

Only when both windows agree is the signal trustworthy.

### The Low-Volume Guard: Minimum Failure Threshold

Here is the caveat that makes burn rate alerting production-safe. A minimum failure threshold — typically 5 actual failures — gates the burn rate detector from firing.

Why? Because 1 error out of 3 requests is a 33% error rate. Without a minimum threshold, that fires the STEEP burn rate detector. But 1 error might be a flaky end-to-end test, a developer hitting a bad endpoint in staging, or a monitoring probe getting an expected error. You do not want to be paged for 1 error at 3am.

The minimum threshold ensures the detector only fires when there is statistically meaningful evidence of user impact.

### Burn Rate Thresholds by SLO Target

Different SLO targets call for different burn rate thresholds. The tighter your SLO, the less budget you have, and the more aggressively you need to detect spending:

<TableCSV source="| SLO Target | Steep Burn | Moderate Burn | Slow Burn |
| 98% | 9× | 5× | 1× |
| 95% | 4× | 2× | 1× |
| 90% | 2× | 1.5× | 1× | "/>

A 90% SLO has more raw budget to spend, so lower multipliers are needed to detect meaningful erosion.

## Part VI: The Latency Pillar — Early Warning Before Errors Appear

Latency gets its own pillar because it behaves differently from error rate. Error rate tells you things are *broken*. Latency tells you things are *degrading* — often before errors start appearing.

**How it is measured:** Histogram-bucketed HTTP response times, alerting at percentile thresholds.

Example thresholds from production systems:
- `/product/hello` → if p95 exceeds 5,000ms → minor alert
- ELB load balancer → if p95 exceeds 2,000ms → observation (no alert, but tracked)
- Chat streaming endpoint → if p95 exceeds 20,000ms → major alert

### Reading the Shape of Latency

Not all latency problems look alike. The shape of the degradation tells you what category of problem you are facing.

**Spike in p95/p99 (sudden, affects high percentiles first):**
This pattern suggests external pressure or circuit-level events. Investigate: downstream rate limiting, circuit breaker open state, a downstream API throwing exceptions or timing out.

**Sudden jump across all percentiles:**
This is the deployment regression pattern. p50, p95, and p99 all step up at the same moment — often correlating with a release. Also caused by upstream errors bleeding into your service's latency.

**Gradual creep over time:**
This is resource saturation in disguise. The service is slowly choking. Common causes: thread pool congestion building up as a slow downstream call holds threads, database connection pool exhaustion, consumer lag growing as the system cannot process fast enough.

For LLM systems, gradual latency creep often signals that model response times are increasing — which can happen due to upstream load, context window size growth, or changes in model behavior under specific prompt patterns.

## Part VII: The Infrastructure Health Pillar — Catching Problems Before Users Do

Infrastructure metrics sit at Level 3 of the monitoring pyramid. They fire before users are impacted, giving you a window to act. Think of them as the warning lights on your car dashboard — the oil pressure light fires before the engine seizes.

### JVM Threads

The JVM thread count is one of the most fundamental health signals in a Java service.

**Thresholds:** 2,000 threads → minor alert. 3,000 threads → major alert.

**What high thread count signals:** Thread leaks from code that creates threads but never terminates them. Blocked I/O where threads are waiting indefinitely on downstream calls. Too many concurrent requests overwhelming the server's capacity. In the worst case, you are approaching an OOM (Out of Memory) error that will kill the process.

### Thread Pool Exhaustion

Modern Java services use async thread pools to handle concurrent requests. When the pool saturates, new requests begin queueing rather than being processed. Latency climbs. Eventually, requests start timing out. Users see slowness before they see errors.

**Thresholds:** 75% utilization → minor alert. 90% utilization → major alert.

**Root causes:** Slow downstream calls holding threads hostage. A database query that used to take 10ms is now taking 2 seconds — every thread that touches the database is occupied 200× longer than expected. The thread pool drains from the top while the leak is at the bottom.

### Tomcat Thread Exhaustion

Tomcat, the embedded servlet container in Spring Boot applications, manages a pool of OS-level threads for handling HTTP connections. This is distinct from your application's async thread pool — Tomcat threads handle the actual I/O.

When Tomcat threads saturate (pre-virtual-thread deployments), the server cannot accept new connections. Requests back up at the load balancer. The service appears to hang rather than error — which is actually harder to debug than a clean 500 error.

### Redis Stream Health

For LLM chat applications that stream responses token-by-token, Redis streams are often the transport layer between the generation service and the user's browser. If Redis stream health degrades, partial responses appear, streams stall mid-generation, and users see incomplete or frozen output.

This is an LLM-specific infrastructure concern that does not exist in traditional request/response architectures. Monitor all Redis stream metrics: consumer lag, message publish rate, consumer group health, connection pool usage.

### Zero Traffic Detection

One of the most insidious failure modes is the absence of signals. A complete outage often produces no errors because nothing is reaching the service to generate them. The load balancer might have misconfigured routing. The service might have crashed silently. DNS might have failed. The SSL certificate might have expired.

**Detection:** Alert if the request rate drops below 1 request in any 30-minute window during expected traffic hours.

This is not a metric about what is happening. It is a metric about what is *not* happening. Zero traffic detectors exist because real production incidents have gone undetected for hours because every signal was silent — because no signal could reach the service to fail.

### HTTPS Certificate Expiration

A special mention for organizations that do not have automated certificate rotation. An expired certificate causes a complete service outage with a particularly confusing error message for users. Certificate expiration dashboards that alert 30, 14, and 7 days before expiry have prevented more than a few silent Sunday night outages.

## Part VIII: LLM-Specific and Domain-Specific Metrics

LLM systems introduce an entire category of metrics that traditional web services never needed. These are not afterthoughts — they are first-class observability concerns.

### Token Usage and Cost

LLM inference is not priced per request. It is priced per token. An LLM service that is functionally healthy (low error rate, acceptable latency) might be generating 10× more tokens per request than expected due to a prompt regression. The cost implications are immediate; the budget implications can be catastrophic at scale.

Track: tokens generated per request, input tokens per request, model distribution (which model is handling traffic), and cost per request derived from token counts.

### Streaming Health

For token-streaming endpoints, batch latency metrics are insufficient. A streaming response has multiple failure modes that aggregate latency does not capture: the stream starting but never delivering the first token, the stream delivering the first token but stalling mid-generation, the stream completing successfully but with latency at the 99th percentile far exceeding the 50th.

Monitor: time-to-first-token, inter-token latency, stream completion rate versus abandonment rate.

### Tool Call and Agent Metrics

For agentic LLM systems — workflows where the model can invoke external tools, make multi-step decisions, and orchestrate complex operations — observability extends to the agent's internal behavior:

**Tool call success and failure rate** — which tools are failing most frequently? A tool that fails 30% of the time is both a reliability problem and a user experience problem, as the agent may retry in ways that increase latency and cost.

**Orchestrator failure rate** — when the agent's planning loop fails, what is the failure mode? Did the model produce malformed JSON? Did the tool call timeout? Did the agent hit a maximum iteration limit?

**Token usage per workflow step** — agents that spiral into unexpected reasoning loops can consume enormous token budgets before failing. Detect this early with per-step token usage tracking.

### Knowledge Base and Retrieval Metrics

For RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) systems: knowledge base hit rate, chunk relevance scores, retrieval latency, and miss rate. A knowledge base miss rate that is rising over time might indicate that your document corpus is becoming stale, that new user query patterns are outside the coverage of your indexed content, or that your embedding model is diverging from production queries.

### Batch Evaluation Health

LLM systems often have offline batch evaluation pipelines — systems that periodically evaluate model quality against golden datasets. These pipelines are not user-facing but are critical for detecting model regressions.

Monitor: job success rate, SQS queue depth (are jobs being processed or backing up?), worker error rate, AI gateway errors from the evaluation pipeline. A batch evaluation system that fails silently leaves you blind to model quality regression.

## Part IX: Resilience Patterns and Their Observability Implications

Resilience patterns are defenses built into the system to handle downstream failures gracefully. They are only as good as your ability to observe them.

### Circuit Breaker

The circuit breaker pattern prevents cascade failures. When a downstream service starts failing, the circuit breaker counts consecutive failures. After a threshold (say, 5 failures per scope), it "opens" — requests to that downstream service fail immediately without attempting the call, for a configurable duration (say, 5 seconds). This prevents a slow, failing downstream from holding your threads hostage and causing a cascade through your system.

**Observability requirement:** You must track circuit breaker state transitions as events and as metrics. A circuit that opens and closes repeatedly is a diagnostic signal — something is intermittently failing downstream. A circuit that opens and stays open is a P1. Without circuit breaker state visibility, you will see the symptoms (elevated latency, rising errors) without the cause.

### Rate Limiting

Tenant-level and user-level rate limiting is essential in LLM systems where inference cost is high and abuse can be financially damaging. Rate limiting decisions must be observable: how many requests are being rate limited per tenant, per user, per bot? A sudden spike in rate limiting on a specific tenant might indicate a runaway agent, a misconfigured automation, or a legitimate but unexpected traffic surge that requires capacity adjustment.

## Part X: The Layers of Detection — Putting It All Together

Great observability systems are not flat. They are layered, with each layer catching a different class of failure. Here is the complete detection architecture from the outermost layer to the innermost:

**Layer 1: Synthetic Monitoring** — Active probes that simulate user behavior on a schedule. These fire even when traffic is zero, and they validate the complete end-to-end path: DNS resolution, load balancer routing, TLS termination, authentication, business logic execution. Synthetic monitoring is the only detection layer that catches infrastructure failures that produce no signals.

**Layer 2: Zero Traffic Detection** — A passive guard that fires when real traffic disappears. This catches partial outages that synthetic probes may not exercise.

**Layer 3: Infrastructure Health Metrics:**
- JVM thread count gauge
- Connection pool utilization
- OS thread count
- Tomcat thread saturation: `AVG(tomcat.threads.busy) / AVG(tomcat.threads.config.max) × 100`
- ELB health (reliability and latency at the load balancer)

**Layer 4: Application Metrics:**
- Endpoint error rate via burn rate SLOs
- Streaming health metrics
- LLM-specific metrics (tokens, model distribution, tool call success)

Each layer is calibrated to a different incident velocity. Synthetic monitoring detects complete outages in under a minute. Infrastructure health metrics detect saturation-driven degradation 5–10 minutes before user impact. Burn rate alerting detects user-impacting failures within 4 minutes for steep burns, 20–30 minutes for moderate burns, and over hours for slow erosion.

## Part XI: Caveats and Failure Modes of Observability Systems

Even well-designed observability systems have failure modes. Here are the ones that bite teams most frequently.

**C1: Synthetic traffic in production metrics.** Synthetic monitoring generates synthetic traffic, and if that traffic is not filtered out, it inflates your request rate metrics and dilutes your error rate. Always tag synthetic requests at the source and filter them from SLO calculations. Real user traffic and synthetic probe traffic have fundamentally different failure characteristics.

**C2: Long-running and async tasks.** Batch jobs, async workflows, and scheduled executions have latency profiles that are 10–100× longer than synchronous user requests. If you apply your synchronous API's p95 latency alert to a batch job that is expected to take 5 minutes, you will be flooded with false positives. The solution is not to filter them out — it is to maintain separate SLOs with appropriate thresholds for different traffic classes.

**C3: Missing metrics are not success signals.** This cannot be overstated. If your metrics pipeline breaks, you will see all your error rates drop to zero and all your latency metrics flatline. This looks exactly like a healthy system. Zero traffic detection exists precisely because teams have been fooled by the silence of a broken observability pipeline into thinking a catastrophic outage was a healthy night.

**C4: Deployment correlation on dashboards.** Every dashboard that shows time-series metrics should also show deployment events as vertical markers. The most common question when investigating an anomaly is "did this correlate with a deployment?" Without that context on the chart, every investigation starts with a manual cross-reference between the metrics timeline and the deployment history.

**C5: The right tags make metrics explorable.** A counter that just counts errors is useful. A counter tagged with `endpoint_path`, `http_method`, `http_status`, `tenant_id`, `bot_id`, and `feature_flag_variant` is an investigation tool. The difference between a 15-minute incident diagnosis and a 2-hour one is usually tag coverage.

## Part XII: What to Log — Structured Logging That Actually Helps

The most powerful incident investigation tool is a well-structured log. The worst is an unstructured log that tells you "ERROR occurred" without any context. Here is the minimum viable log schema:

**Request Identity:**
- `requestId` — a unique identifier that correlates this log line with every other log line from the same request, across services. Include `transactionId`, `traceId`, and `spanId` to connect logs to traces.

**Context Data:**
- `partitionId`, `shardId`, `serviceId` — where in the system did this happen?
- `userId`, `tenantId`, `botId` — who triggered this?

**Outcome:**
- `httpStatus` — what was the result?
- `circuitBreakerStatus` — was the circuit breaker involved?
- `featureFlagName`, `featureFlagStatus` — was this request subject to a feature flag that might explain the behavior?

**Performance:**
- `latencyMs` — how long did this specific operation take?
- `operationCount` — how many items were processed?

**LLM-Specific:**
- `inputTokens`, `outputTokens` — how much did this call cost?
- `modelId` — which model handled the request?
- `streamingEnabled` — was this a streaming call?

The principle behind this schema is that every meaningful dimension of the request — identity, context, outcome, and performance — should be available for filtering and grouping in your log search tool. When you are investigating an incident at 2am, you want to be able to type `tenantId=acme AND httpStatus=500 AND circuitBreakerStatus=OPEN` and get the exact log lines that explain the failure.

## Part XIII: Implementation Checklist

Building a production-grade observability system is a project, not an afternoon task. Here is the ordered implementation path:

1. **Instrument your code.** Add Micrometer counters and timers to every meaningful code path — HTTP endpoints, LLM calls, database queries, tool invocations, cache operations. This is the foundation everything else depends on.

2. **Add tags to every metric.** Tags are what transform metrics from aggregate noise into a diagnostic instrument. At minimum: endpoint path, HTTP method, HTTP status, tenant ID, service ID. For LLM-specific metrics: model ID, streaming flag, tool name.

3. **Build domain dashboards.** One dashboard per concern: endpoint reliability, latency profiles, infrastructure health, LLM-specific metrics (token usage, model distribution, streaming health), agent/workflow health.

4. **Add deployment markers to every dashboard.** Every time-series chart should show deploy events as vertical lines.

5. **Implement zero traffic detection.** This protects against the complete failure modes that generate no other signals.

6. **Configure burn rate SLOs.** Start with your most critical endpoints. Define the SLO target, configure the three burn rate tiers with appropriate window pairs, set the minimum failure threshold.

7. **Add infrastructure saturation alerts.** Thread pool utilization, JVM thread count, Redis stream health. These are the early warning layer that fires before burn rate does.

8. **Implement synthetic monitoring.** Scheduled end-to-end probes that validate the full user flow.

9. **Set up error tracking (Sentry).** Burn rate alerting tells you *that* errors are happening. Sentry tells you *what* errors they are, grouped and deduplicated so you can see the top 3 error patterns instead of 10,000 individual stack traces.

10. **Test your alerting.** Deliberately degrade your service in a non-production environment and verify that the right alerts fire at the right severity at the right time. An alert that fires in production for the first time during an actual incident is an untested alert.

## Closing: The Philosophy Behind the Practice

Observability is not a compliance requirement or a box-checking exercise. It is the operational expression of taking your users' experience seriously.

Every alert threshold represents a judgment call: at what point does this metric signal user impact? Every SLO target is a public commitment: we will be reliable at least this often. Every structured log field is preparation for a future investigation you cannot yet anticipate.

The burn rate model, the multi-window dual detector, the layered detection architecture — these are not academic constructs. They are the product of engineers getting paged at 2am, learning from what they missed, and encoding that knowledge into their systems.
Build observability as if you will be the one on call. Because you probably will be.

**Key Takeaways:**

1. LLM observability requires semantic monitoring — traditional p99 latency is not enough.
2. Trace every stage (guardrail → retrieval → inference → validation) independently.
3. Log every prompt and response (with PII redaction) for debugging and compliance.
4. Track quality scores, not just error rates — a coherent-sounding wrong answer is the hardest bug to catch.
5. Cost attribution per-request enables informed model-tiering decisions.
